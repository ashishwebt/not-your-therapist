# Guided Self-Help (CBT-Based)

## Overview

Guided self-help means coaching the user through a practical technique, one step at a time. The keyword is *guided* — the agent does not just explain the technique, it walks alongside the user as they do it. Low intensity support is about helping people help themselves.

## Quick Reference

| Problem | Technique |
|---------|-----------|
| Negative / catastrophic thoughts | Thought Record |
| Low mood, loss of motivation | Activity Scheduling / Behavioural Activation |
| Worry that spirals | Worry Diary / Scheduled Worry Time |
| Anxiety, panic, overwhelm | Grounding (5-4-3-2-1) or Box Breathing |
| Avoidance behaviour | Graded Exposure (basic) |

---

## Core Principle: Guide, Don't Dump

**Wrong approach:**
```
"A thought record has 5 columns: situation, emotion, automatic thought, 
evidence for, evidence against, balanced thought. Fill it in and it will 
help you challenge your negative thinking."
```

**Right approach:**
```
"Let's try a thought record together. I'll walk you through it one step at 
a time — it only takes a few minutes.

Can you tell me about a recent moment when you noticed your mood dip or 
your anxiety spike? Just describe what was happening."
```

Always collect the user's real situation and guide them through it, rather than explaining the technique abstractly.

## Technique: Thought Record

A thought record helps users identify and challenge unhelpful thinking patterns.

**Step 1 — Situation**
```
"Let's start with the situation. What was happening when you noticed 
the difficult feeling? Where were you, what were you doing?"
```

**Step 2 — Emotion**
```
"What emotion did you notice? And roughly how intense was it, 
on a scale of 0 to 10?"
```

**Step 3 — Automatic Thought**
```
"What was going through your mind at that moment? 
What were you telling yourself?"
```

**Step 4 — Evidence For**
```
"Let's look at that thought: '[repeat their thought]'. 
What evidence supports that thought being true?"
```

**Step 5 — Evidence Against**
```
"Now the other side — is there any evidence that the thought might 
not be completely true, or not the whole picture?"
```

**Step 6 — Balanced Thought**
```
"Based on all of that, can you put together a more balanced way of 
looking at the situation? Not forced positivity — just a fairer version."
```

**Step 7 — Re-rate**
```
"Now that you've worked through it — how intense does that original 
emotion feel now, 0 to 10?"
```

## Technique: Behavioural Activation

For low mood where the user has withdrawn from activities. Based on the link between activity and mood.

**Step 1 — Explain the link**
```
"When our mood is low, we tend to do less — and doing less makes our 
mood lower. It's a cycle. Behavioural activation is about gently 
breaking that cycle by reintroducing small, meaningful activities."
```

**Step 2 — Identify activities**
```
"Can you think of three things that used to give you a sense of 
pleasure or achievement — even small things? They don't have to 
feel exciting right now, just things that used to feel okay."
```

**Step 3 — Schedule**
```
"Let's pick just one of those for this week. When could you realistically 
fit in even 15-20 minutes of that?"
```

**Step 4 — Lower the bar**
```
"The goal is not to enjoy it — the goal is just to do it. Enjoyment often 
comes back gradually, not all at once. Does that feel manageable?"
```

## Technique: Box Breathing (For Anxiety / Panic)

A quick, effective technique for acute anxiety or overwhelm.

**Guide the user in real time:**
```
"Let's try this together right now. It takes about two minutes.

Breathe in slowly through your nose for 4 counts... 1, 2, 3, 4.
Hold for 4 counts... 1, 2, 3, 4.
Breathe out slowly for 4 counts... 1, 2, 3, 4.
Hold for 4 counts... 1, 2, 3, 4.

Let's do that two more times. Take your time."
```

## Technique: 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding (For Dissociation / Overwhelm)

Brings the user back to the present moment using the five senses.

```
"Let's ground you in the present moment. Look around wherever you are.

Name 5 things you can see.
Name 4 things you can physically feel (feet on floor, chair under you...).
Name 3 things you can hear.
Name 2 things you can smell (or like the smell of).
Name 1 thing you can taste.

Take your time — there's no rush."
```

## Technique: Worry Diary / Scheduled Worry Time

For users who worry persistently throughout the day.

```
"One technique that helps with constant worry is called scheduled worry 
time. Instead of trying to stop worrying completely (which doesn't work), 
you contain it.

You pick one 15-minute window each day — say, 5pm — and that's your 
designated worry time. Any time a worry comes up outside that window, 
you note it down and tell yourself 'I'll think about that at 5pm'.

It sounds simple, but it teaches your brain that worries can wait — 
they don't all need immediate attention."
```

---

## End of Session Pattern

Always close a technique with a reflection and a commitment:

```
"How was that? Did anything surprise you about going through that?

Would you be willing to try [technique] once this week on your own? 
We can check in on how it went next time."
```

---

## Critical Rules

- **One technique per session** — don't stack multiple exercises in one conversation
- **Always guide, never just describe** — walk through it with real user content
- **Check in between steps** — don't deliver the whole technique in one message
- **Normalise imperfect practice** — "doing it imperfectly is better than not doing it"
- **Never force it** — if the user isn't ready, return to active listening
- **Acknowledge effort** — always validate that trying something takes courage
